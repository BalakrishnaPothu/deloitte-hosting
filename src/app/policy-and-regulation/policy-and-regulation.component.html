Senate bills seek to reform commercial space regulations | 29-March-2024

The Commercial Standards Paramount to Accelerating Cosmic Exploration (SPACE) Leadership Act would extend the learning period by five years, improving the ability of the authorities to enact regulations for the safety of occupants on commercial spacecraft.

The second bill directs authorities to streamline the commercial launch licensing process by incorporating industry input.

The overall aim of the two bills is to cut red tape to make commercial space launches and licensing processes more efficient. The private and commercial space industry seems to welcome the progress on this front as it would allow them to drive and provide input on future commercial human space flight regulations, and improve innovation and competitiveness in space.

USA
Senate passes the Orbital Sustainability Act of 2023 or the Orbits Act of 2023 | 31-October-2023

The main aim of the Act is to direct specific agencies to conduct active debris remediation or removal of human-made space objects that are no longer in use and can harm orbiting satellites and on-orbit activities. The remediation is defined as "deliberate process of facilitating the de-orbit, repurposing, or other disposal of orbital debris". The Act passed in the Senate and is now received in the House.

Clients who have the technology to aid the agencies in the remediation could potentially submit proposals to NASA once the demonstration program is launched. Meanwhile, clients who have launched space objects should be informed that orbit debris may be identified by the government for removal.

Thailand
A general introduction to Space Law in Thailand | 22nd December 2023

At present, Thailand has not enacted a comprehensive national space law to regulate space affairs and activities in support of its space sector. Such affairs include the registration of space objects and the management of accidents in space. However, Thailand does have agencies that cover such matters, the most significant of which is the Geo-Informatics and Space Technology Development Agency (GISTDA), a government agency assigned by the Cabinet of Thailand (the Cabinet) to manage space affairs and activities. The GISTDA does not oversee or establish centralized, comprehensive policies and regulations.

In addition, Thailand has a presence in space with its own satellites for telecommunications and the exploration of natural resources. Various agencies have been (or will be) established that are responsible for satellite-related affairs, for example, the GISTDA and the National Space Policy Committee (NSPC).

Thailand's entry into the global space sector anticipates the integration of over 50 Thai companies into the industry's supply chain within seven years. The impact transcends traditional space applications, extending to electronics, automation, agriculture, mining, construction, medical, and tourism sectors. This initiative promises technological progress and significant economic expansion for Thailand. The government's proactive engagement in international agreements and domestic legislation reflects its commitment to space technology development and global cooperation. Thailand aims to bolster its position in the international space industry, capitalizing on its emerging status as a key player in the global space arena.

Russia, US
Russia vetoes UN Security Council Resolution proposed by US and Japan | 24th April 2024

Russia exercised its veto power in the UN Security Council, blocking a resolution proposed by the US and Japan. The resolution aimed to uphold Article 4 of the Outer Space Treaty, prohibiting the placement of nuclear weapons in orbit or on celestial bodies, and preventing the development of weapons of mass destruction specifically designed for space deployment.

The resolution received support from 13 other members of the Security Council, while China abstained from voting. However, Russia's veto prevented its adoption. It was reported that Russia and China proposed an amendment to the resolution, aiming to prevent the placement of any weapons in space, but this amendment failed to pass.

The disagreement within the UN Security Council highlights differing perspectives on upholding the provisions of the Outer Space Treaty regarding nuclear weapons in space. This situation poses a challenge to the council's enforcement capabilities and raises questions about the effectiveness of the treaty.
