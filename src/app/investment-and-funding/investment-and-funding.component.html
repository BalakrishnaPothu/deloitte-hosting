Kominfo unveils plans for the Satria-2 Satellite funded through foreign investment | 13th March 2024

The Ministry of Communication and Informatics is leading the development of the Satria-2 satellite, aiming for a capacity of 300 Gbps. This ambitious project is entirely financed by foreign investment, with an estimated budget of US$ 860 million (IDR 13 trillion). Discussions regarding technical aspects are ongoing with key government agencies such as the National Development Planning Agency (Bappenas) and the Ministry of Finance. The decision on whether the satellite will operate in Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO) or Low Earth Orbit (LEO) is pending. Once operational, the satellite's internet services will benefit various sectors including education, healthcare, government services, security, and tourism. Procurement is scheduled to begin in 2025, building upon the success of the Satria-1 satellite launched in 2023. The orbit scheme for Satria-2 will be determined through a competitive tendering process, involving satellite companies such as Satelit Nusantara Tiga (SNT).

The announcement of the Satria-2 satellite project underscores Indonesia's commitment to utilizing international investment for technological progress. With its substantial capacity and focus on critical sectors, the satellite promises to elevate communities and enhance economic productivity nationwide. By targeting remote and underserved regions, inaccessible via traditional means, Indonesia aims to narrow the digital divide and promote inclusivity. The transparent procurement process and involvement of private satellite companies reflect a collaborative approach to innovation and development. Overall, the Satria-2 satellite initiative heralds a transformative phase for Indonesia, offering unprecedented connectivity and opportunities for socioeconomic advancement.

India
India Boosts Space Sector Investment with Expanded Foreign Funding Limits | 22nd February 2024

India has revised its foreign direct investment (FDI) policy in the space sector, allowing increased overseas investment across different segments. The amended policy, approved by the Indian cabinet, defines three categories with varying degrees of foreign ownership limits, ranging from 49% to 100%. These include launch vehicles, satellite manufacturing, and components for satellites and ground segments. Investments beyond specified thresholds require government approval, aiming to attract global investors and spur growth in the Indian space industry.

The updated FDI norms are expected to stimulate increased investment and interest in India's space industry, fostering innovation, job creation, and economic growth. By easing restrictions and offering greater foreign ownership opportunities, India aims to accelerate the development of its space technology ecosystem. This move aligns with India's broader strategy to enhance its presence in the global space economy, leveraging its growing ecosystem of space startups and private companies.

Japan to create ¥1 trillion fund to develop outer space industry | 13th November 2023

The Japanese government is planning to establish a new ¥1 trillion ($6.6 billion) fund over a 10-year period to develop the country’s outer space industry, with ¥300 billion allocated in the latest supplementary budget. The fund aims to accelerate Japan’s space development efforts amid intensifying international competition. The funding will support the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) and the growth of Japan’s space industry, responding to increased focus on space activities in both the public and private sectors. Japan’s Space Basic Plan focuses on budgetary support for private sector innovation, while the Space Security Initiative underscores the geopolitical importance of space.

"The funding aims to bolster Japan's presence in the global space economy by accelerating space development and enhancing competitiveness in the space race. This investment signifies Japan's commitment to leveraging space technology for economic growth and scientific advancement. Accelerating Space Development: The infusion of ¥1 trillion over a 10-year period will accelerate Japan's space development efforts (JAXA projects and initiatives). Competing in Global Space Race: With countries around the world actively pursuing space exploration and development projects, the fund will help Japan remain competitive in the global space race. This is essential given geopolitical developments as major superpowers like the United States and China intensify their efforts to establish a presence in outer space."

US wants NZ to stump up cash for $1.6b military satellite project | 20th December 2023

The United States is embroiled in a multi-billion dollar space race with China and Russia, focusing on military satellite technology for communication, jamming, tracking, and surveillance. As part of this effort, the US is pressuring its allies, including New Zealand, to contribute more funding to a $1.6 billion project to launch two powerful new military satellites. New Zealand currently relies on aging US satellites and has only committed $15 million towards launching one of the replacements.

This request for increased funding presents a difficult choice for New Zealand. The country's Defence Force is already grappling with fundamental issues like understaffing and outdated equipment. The government is hesitant to boost defense spending, particularly in light of the current economic climate. Contributing more to the US project would solidify New Zealand's alignment with the US and its military interests. However, developing its own independent space capabilities would lessen reliance on the US but likely come at a significantly higher cost.
