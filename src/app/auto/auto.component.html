Plants in Space to address sustainability challenges | 10th May 2024

The article discusses Australia's initiative to develop agriculture in space to address both sustainability challenges on Earth, as well as the feasibility of space exploration. By growing plants in space, researchers hope to learn more about how they respond to microgravity and other space conditions, which could inform future space missions and even help establish human colonies off-Earth. The research is led by The Australian Research Council (ARC) Centre of Excellence in Plants for Space (P4S), administered by the University of Adelaide, and supported by the Australian Space Agency.

Potential clients: Australia is becoming a world leader in space crops and agriculture. Innovative bioresource research worth noting includes University of Adelaide’s Plants for Space (P4S) and Lunaria One in Melbourne.

Potential cross-industries/functions:
1) Making extended human space exploration and colonies a possibility, reducing the dependency on Earth “re-fuelling” of food and medicine.
2) Making food production more sustainable and resilient on Earth.

Space Spotlight: Moving spacecraft with mothball power | 27th February 2024

An Australian company, Boswell Technologies, pioneers the use of mothballs, specifically naphthalene, as propellant for satellites with their Bogong Thruster. Inspired by a PhD student's research, the thruster's simplicity and effectiveness offer advantages over traditional propellants. Custom valve design challenges were overcome, leading to a successful in-orbit demonstration aboard a Skykraft Block III satellite, marking a milestone in propulsion technology.

The Bogong Thruster's successful demonstration prompts interest from major satellite companies like Skykraft, leading to commercial partnerships and sales. Boswell Technologies, supported by the Australian National University, now aims to capitalize on the technology's success by targeting commercial contracts, particularly in the small satellite and CubeSat market, bolstering innovation in space propulsion.

Cow-Dung Powers Japan's New Rocket Engine Prototype, Advances in Sustainable Space Tech | 3rd April 2024

The article highlights Japan's pioneering use of cow dung-derived biofuel to power rockets, marking a significant advancement in sustainable space exploration. Scientists successfully conducted a test flight using this eco-friendly fuel, demonstrating its viability for future missions. By extracting methane from cow dung, Japan aims to reduce reliance on traditional rocket fuels, mitigate environmental impact, and lower costs. This innovative approach showcases Japan's commitment to eco-conscious technology and sets a precedent for greener space exploration worldwide.

This article underscores the transformative potential of sustainable biofuels in space exploration. Japan's use of cow dung-derived biofuel represents a paradigm shift towards greener propulsion methods, with implications far beyond the aerospace industry. By demonstrating the feasibility of eco-friendly rocket fuel, Japan sets a precedent for other nations to explore alternative, renewable energy sources for space missions. This innovation could significantly reduce the environmental footprint of space exploration, addressing concerns about carbon emissions and resource depletion. Moreover, it highlights the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration, as scientists, engineers, and agricultural experts work together to harness the power of biofuels. Ultimately, this initiative represents a crucial step towards a more sustainable future for humanity's endeavors beyond Earth, inspiring further research and development in environmentally-friendly space technologies.

Nuclear fusion reactor in South Korea runs at 100 million degrees C for a record-breaking 48 seconds | 14th April 2024

The article reports on South Korea's successful operation of a nuclear fusion reactor for 48 seconds, breaking the previous world record of 31 seconds. The KSTAR (Korea Superconducting Tokamak Advanced Research) reactor, also known as an “artificial sun”, reached a plasma temperature of over 100 million degrees Celsius, maintaining it for nearly a minute. This achievement is crucial in the pursuit of nuclear fusion energy, which holds immense potential as a safe, abundant, and clean power source.

Nuclear fusion replicates the process that powers the sun, fusing hydrogen atoms into helium, releasing vast amounts of energy in the process. The successful operation of the KSTAR reactor demonstrates progress in controlling and sustaining fusion reactions, essential for future energy generation. South Korea's advancements contribute to global efforts in harnessing fusion as a sustainable energy solution, though significant challenges remain before fusion can be commercially viable.
